0. Herodotus of Halicarnassus wrote this account to ensure that the remarkable achievements of both Greeks and non-Greeks are not lost to time. He sought to preserve the glory of their great and wondrous deeds, including the reasons behind their wars with each other. 
1. According to Persian scholars, the Phoenicians were responsible for the conflict. They came to our seas from the Red Sea and settled in the land they still inhabit today, immediately embarking on long voyages. They transported goods from Egypt and Assyria to various locations, including Argos, which was then the most prominent city among the people of what is now known as Greece. Upon arriving in Argos, the Phoenicians unloaded their cargo. Five or six days later, when most of their goods had been sold, a group of women, including the daughter of the king named Io (as both Persians and Greeks agree), arrived at the shore. While the women were bargaining for the goods they desired at the back of the ship, the Phoenicians encouraged each other to attack them. Although most of the women managed to flee, Io and a few others were captured and taken aboard the ship, which then sailed off towards Egypt. 
2. According to the Persians, Io's arrival in Egypt was not a voluntary one, unlike what the Greeks believe. They consider this to be the first act of wrongdoing. The Persians recount that some Greeks, whose identity they are unsure of, arrived in Phoenicia and abducted the king's daughter, Europa. It is believed that these Greeks were likely from Crete. This event balanced the account between the two cultures until the Greeks committed a second wrong. They traveled to the city of Aea in Colchis and took the king's daughter, Medea, after completing their mission. Upon receiving a demand for reparation and the return of his daughter from the Colchian king, the Greeks refused to comply. They cited the lack of reparation for the abduction of Io, a woman from Argos, as their reason for not making any amends to the Colchians.

The Greeks initially decided to send messengers to demand the return of Helen and seek compensation for her capture. 6. Croesus, born in Lydia and son of Alyattes, ruled over all the nations to the west of the Halys River. This river flows from the south between Syria and Paphlagonia, and eventually empties into the Euxine Sea. Croesus was the first foreigner on record to conquer and demand tribute from certain Greek nations, while also earning the loyalty of others. The Ionians, Aeolians, and Dorians of Asia fell under his subjugation, while the Lacedaemonians became his allies. Prior to Croesus' reign, all Greeks enjoyed freedom.
